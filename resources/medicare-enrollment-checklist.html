Medicare Enrollment Checklist

Your step-by-step guide to enrolling with confidence — prepared by Balcones Advisors

1. Know Your Enrollment Windows

WindowWhenWhat You Can Do
Initial Enrollment Period (IEP)3 months before → 3 months after your 65th birthday monthEnroll in Parts A, B, C, D; buy Medigap
Annual Enrollment (AEP)October 15 – December 7 each yearSwitch Medicare Advantage or Part D plans
Open Enrollment (OEP)January 1 – March 31 each yearSwitch from Medicare Advantage to Original Medicare
Special Enrollment (SEP)Qualifying life event (e.g., moving, losing employer coverage)Enroll or change plans outside normal windows
⚠️ Don't miss your IEP. Late enrollment in Part B can result in a 10% penalty for each 12-month period you were eligible but didn't enroll — and this penalty lasts for life.

2. Before You Enroll — Gather These

  • Social Security number
  • Date you (or your spouse) stopped working, if applicable
  • Current health insurance details (carrier, group number, ID)
  • List of current prescription medications (name, dosage, frequency)
  • List of your current doctors and specialists
  • Any VA, TRICARE, or employer coverage documentation
  • Proof of U.S. citizenship or lawful residency

3. Understand Your Options

Original Medicare (Parts A + B)

  • Part A: Hospital insurance (usually premium-free if you paid Medicare taxes 10+ years)
  • Part B: Medical insurance (doctor visits, outpatient, preventive). Standard premium: check Medicare.gov for current year
  • You choose any Medicare-accepting doctor nationwide
  • Consider a Medigap (Supplement) plan to cover deductibles and copays
  • You'll need a separate Part D plan for prescription drugs

Medicare Advantage (Part C)

  • Bundles Parts A, B, and usually D into one plan
  • Often includes dental, vision, hearing, and fitness benefits
  • Uses a provider network (HMO, PPO, or PFFS)
  • Compare: monthly premium, copays, max out-of-pocket, drug formulary
  • Check that your doctors and preferred hospital are in-network

Medigap (Medicare Supplement)

  • Covers costs Original Medicare doesn't (deductibles, coinsurance, copays)
  • Plans are standardized by letter (A, B, D, G, K, L, M, N) — benefits are the same regardless of carrier
  • Best time to buy: during your 6-month Medigap Open Enrollment (starts the month you're 65+ AND enrolled in Part B)
  • During this window, carriers cannot deny you or charge more for pre-existing conditions
💡 Pro tip: The best Medigap rates are locked in during your Open Enrollment window. After that, carriers can medically underwrite you. Don't wait.

4. Compare Plans — What to Look At

  • Monthly premium
  • Annual deductible
  • Maximum out-of-pocket limit
  • Copays for doctor visits, specialists, and hospital stays
  • Prescription drug coverage: Is your pharmacy in-network? Are your meds on the formulary?
  • Star rating (1–5 stars, rated by CMS for quality)
  • Extra benefits: dental, vision, hearing, telehealth, OTC allowance

5. Enrollment Day — Action Items

  • Enroll in Part A and Part B via ssa.gov or your local Social Security office
  • Choose: Original Medicare + Medigap + Part D, OR Medicare Advantage
  • If choosing Medigap, apply during your 6-month guaranteed-issue window
  • Set up your Medicare.gov account to manage benefits, find plans, and track claims
  • Confirm your primary care doctor accepts your new coverage
  • Set a calendar reminder for AEP (Oct 15 – Dec 7) to review your plan annually

6. After You Enroll

  • Keep your Medicare card in a safe place (and take a photo for backup)
  • Review your first Medicare Summary Notice (MSN) for accuracy
  • Watch for your new plan's welcome materials and ID card
  • Schedule your free "Welcome to Medicare" preventive visit within 12 months of enrollment
  • Review your plan every year during AEP — plans change, and so do your needs
